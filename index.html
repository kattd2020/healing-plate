High-Calorie Vanilla Milkshake

Nausea Mouth Sores No Appetite Liquid

Serves 1  |  Ready in 5 minutes  |  Blender

Chemo-friendly: no raw eggs, easy on the stomach, and gentle enough for mouth sores. Served cold to help soothe nausea.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup whole milk
  • 2 scoops vanilla ice cream
  • 2 tbsp heavy cream
  • 2 tbsp powdered whole milk
  • 1 tbsp honey
  • 1 tsp pure vanilla extract
  • Pinch of salt

Instructions

  1. Add all ingredients to a blender.
  2. Blend on medium for 30 seconds until smooth and creamy.
  3. Pour into a chilled glass.
  4. Sip slowly through a straw if mouth sores are present.
~620 calories 14g protein 38g fat 55g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Stir in 1 tbsp unsalted butter after blending — adds ~100 cal and richness
  • Swap milk for an extra 2 tbsp heavy cream — adds ~100 cal
  • Blend in 2 tbsp peanut or almond butter — adds ~190 cal and 7g protein
  • Use sweetened condensed milk instead of regular milk — boosts calories and sweetness
  • Add a scoop of vanilla protein powder — adds ~110 cal and 20g protein

Banana Ginger Smoothie

Nausea No Appetite Liquid

Serves 1  |  Ready in 5 minutes  |  Blender

Ginger is one of the most well-studied natural remedies for chemo-related nausea. Banana adds gentle sweetness and is easy on a sensitive stomach.

Ingredients

  • 1 ripe banana (frozen is ideal)
  • ½ cup vanilla yogurt
  • ½ cup whole milk
  • ¼ tsp ground ginger (or ½ tsp fresh, grated)
  • 1 tbsp honey
  • ½ tsp vanilla extract
  • 4–5 ice cubes

Instructions

  1. Add all ingredients to a blender.
  2. Blend on high for 45 seconds until smooth.
  3. Taste and add more honey if needed.
  4. Pour into a chilled glass and sip slowly.
~320 calories 8g protein 5g fat 63g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Add 2 tbsp peanut or almond butter — adds ~190 cal and 7g protein
  • Swap milk for heavy cream — adds ~100 cal
  • Blend in 2 tbsp powdered whole milk — adds ~80 cal and extra protein
  • Add 1 tbsp coconut oil — adds ~120 cal of healthy fat

Creamy Potato Leek Soup

Nausea Mouth Sores No Appetite Soft

Serves 4  |  Ready in 35 minutes  |  Stovetop + Blender

Bland and buttery — one of the safest soups for all chemo symptoms. Blended smooth for mouth sores, served warm (not hot) to avoid irritation.

Ingredients

  • 3 medium Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled and diced
  • 2 leeks, white and light green parts, sliced
  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 4 cups low-sodium chicken or vegetable broth
  • ½ cup heavy cream
  • ½ tsp salt
  • White pepper to taste (optional)

Instructions

  1. Melt butter in a pot over medium-low heat. Add leeks and cook 8 minutes until very soft.
  2. Add potatoes and broth. Bring to a gentle simmer.
  3. Cook 20 minutes until potatoes are completely tender.
  4. Blend until silky smooth. Stir in cream.
  5. Season gently and serve warm, not hot.
~380 calories 8g protein 22g fat 38g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Stir in an extra tbsp of butter per bowl — adds ~100 cal
  • Replace some broth with heavy cream — significantly boosts fat and calories
  • Top with a swirl of sour cream — adds ~60 cal and richness
  • Blend in 2 tbsp cream cheese for a velvety texture and extra calories

Pureed Butternut Squash Soup

Nausea No Appetite Liquid

Serves 4  |  Ready in 40 minutes  |  Stovetop + Blender

Naturally sweet and very mild — easy on a nauseous stomach. The silky texture requires no chewing and goes down easily even when appetite is very low.

Ingredients

  • 1 medium butternut squash, peeled and cubed
  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 3 cups low-sodium chicken broth
  • ½ cup heavy cream
  • ¼ tsp nutmeg
  • ½ tsp salt

Instructions

  1. Melt butter in a pot over medium heat. Add squash and cook 5 minutes.
  2. Add broth and simmer 25 minutes until squash is very tender.
  3. Blend completely smooth.
  4. Stir in cream and nutmeg. Warm gently — do not boil.
  5. Serve at a comfortable warm temperature.
~300 calories 5g protein 18g fat 30g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Blend in 2 tbsp peanut butter — adds ~190 cal (pairs surprisingly well)
  • Stir in extra butter per serving — adds ~100 cal
  • Use full-fat coconut milk instead of cream for a different flavor and similar calories
  • Swirl in 2 tbsp sour cream per bowl — adds ~60 cal

Creamy Chicken Noodle Soup

Nausea No Appetite Soft

Serves 4  |  Ready in 35 minutes  |  Stovetop

A nourishing classic made richer with cream. The noodles are cooked extra soft and chicken is shredded fine — no tough pieces to chew. High protein to support healing.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups egg noodles, cooked very soft
  • 4 cups low-sodium chicken broth
  • ½ cup heavy cream

Buttery Mashed Potatoes

Mouth Sores No Appetite Soft

Serves 4  |  Ready in 30 minutes  |  Stovetop

Smooth, creamy, and completely easy to swallow. No lumps, no skin, no hard pieces — safe even with significant mouth sores. One of the most comforting soft foods during treatment.

Ingredients

  • 4 large Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled and cubed
  • 4 tbsp unsalted butter
  • ½ cup heavy cream, warmed
  • ¼ cup whole milk, warmed
  • ½ tsp salt

Instructions

  1. Boil potatoes in salted water until very tender, about 20 minutes.
  2. Drain well and return to pot over low heat for 1 minute to dry.
  3. Mash thoroughly, then beat in butter, cream, and milk.
  4. Pass through a ricer or fine mesh strainer for a silky, lump-free texture.
  5. Serve warm — not hot — if mouth is sensitive.
~380 calories 5g protein 20g fat 46g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Add an extra tbsp of butter per serving — adds ~100 cal
  • Stir in 2 tbsp cream cheese — adds ~100 cal and extra creaminess
  • Use sour cream in place of milk — adds richness and ~60 cal per tbsp
  • Blend in 2 tbsp powdered whole milk — adds ~80 cal and protein

Soft Scrambled Eggs with Cream Cheese

Mouth Sores No Appetite Soft

Serves 1  |  Ready in 10 minutes  |  Stovetop

Cooked very low and slow so they stay silky and custardy — never rubbery. A high-protein option that's gentle on a sore mouth and easy to eat in small bites.

Ingredients

  • 3 large eggs
  • 2 tbsp cream cheese, softened
  • 1 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 1 tbsp whole milk or cream
  • Pinch of salt

Instructions

  1. Whisk eggs, milk, and salt together until well combined.
  2. Melt butter in a non-stick pan over the lowest heat possible.
  3. Add eggs and stir slowly and constantly with a spatula.
  4. Pull off heat just before fully set — residual heat will finish them.
  5. Fold in cream cheese off heat and stir until melted through.
~310 calories 18g protein 24g fat 2g carbs

Make It Higher Calorie

  • Use heavy cream instead of milk — adds ~50 cal
  • Add an extra tbsp of butter — adds ~100 cal
  • Increase cream cheese to 3 tbsp — adds ~100 cal
  • Serve over soft white toast with butter for a full 500+ cal meal

Homemade Vanilla Pudding

Nausea Mouth Sores No Appetite Soft

Serves 2  |  Ready in 15 min + chill  |  Stovetop

Cool, silky, and soothing — homemade pudding has no artificial ingredients or harsh additives found in packaged versions. Serve chilled for mouth sore comfort or at room temperature for nausea.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups whole milk
  • ⅓ cup granulated sugar
  • 3 tbsp cornstarch

🍳 Helping with Food & Nutrition

  • Offer small amounts of food every 2–3 hours rather than pushing three full meals. A few bites at a time adds up.
  • Keep easy, ready-to-eat options on hand — yogurt, pudding, crackers with peanut butter — so eating requires no effort from them.
  • Cook with as little smell as possible on hard days. Cold foods and room-temperature foods produce far less odor than hot meals.
  • Let them choose. Even if their requests seem odd or limited, honoring food preferences helps maintain a sense of control.
  • Add butter, cream, or nut butter to foods quietly without making it a production — just boost calories where you can.
  • Keep a water bottle, herbal tea, or diluted juice nearby at all times. Sipping throughout the day prevents dehydration without feeling overwhelming.
  • Note which foods they tolerated well and which caused issues. Patterns emerge over treatment cycles and you can plan ahead.
  • Avoid strong-smelling foods like garlic, onion, fish, and cruciferous vegetables on nausea days.

🩸 Managing Mouth Sores & Sensitivity

  • Rinse their mouth with a saltwater or baking soda rinse before meals — it clears bacteria and reduces pain while eating.
  • Serve foods lukewarm or cool. Hot foods irritate inflamed tissue significantly.
  • Avoid acidic foods (citrus, tomato, vinegar) and anything crunchy, sharp, or rough-edged.
  • Use a soft-bristle toothbrush or a foam swab if regular brushing is too painful.
  • Ask their oncology team about prescription mouth rinses if sores become severe — this is very manageable with the right treatment.

💦 Managing Nausea

  • Offer food before nausea peaks. Many patients feel best in the morning before fatigue and medication side effects build up.
  • Ginger tea, ginger chews, or ginger in smoothies is one of the most effective natural nausea aids — keep it stocked.
  • Cold and room-temperature foods are almost always better tolerated than hot foods on nausea days.
  • Encourage eating in a well-ventilated room away from cooking smells.
  • Small, frequent bites work better than sitting down to a full plate. Remove the plate if they feel overwhelmed by it.
  • Anti-nausea medications are most effective when taken on schedule — help them stay consistent with timing.

❤️ Emotional Support

  • Don't make food a battleground. Pressure and anxiety around eating makes things worse. Gentle encouragement is enough.
  • Sit with them while they eat, even if you're just having a cup of tea. Company makes meals feel less like a chore.
  • Ask "what sounds good to you?" instead of presenting a full meal. Having a choice — even a tiny one — matters a lot.
  • Acknowledge bad days without trying to fix them. Sometimes "that sounds really hard" is more helpful than solutions.
  • Celebrate small wins. Two bites of oatmeal on a terrible nausea day is genuinely worth acknowledging.
  • Don't take food refusals personally. Loss of appetite is a physical symptom, not a rejection of your effort.
  • Ask them directly what kind of support they want — some people want distraction and conversation, others want quiet. Check in rather than assuming.
  • Let them feel whatever they feel. You don't need to reframe everything as positive. Being present is enough.

🪑 Taking Care of Yourself

  • Caregiver burnout is real. You cannot pour from an empty cup — your health matters too.
  • Accept help when it's offered. Let others bring meals, run errands, or sit with your loved one so you can rest.
  • Find one person you can talk to honestly about how you're doing — a friend, therapist, or caregiver support group.
  • It's okay to feel frustrated, scared, or grief-stricken. These feelings don't make you a bad caregiver.
  • Batch-cook when you have energy. Having soups or soft foods ready in the freezer reduces daily pressure enormously.
  • Ask the oncology team what to expect in upcoming treatment cycles so you can prepare rather than react.
